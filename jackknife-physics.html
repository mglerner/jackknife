The Jackknife

Kinematics and stability of backing a vehicle with a trailer — a classical-mechanics case study, and the model behind the Jackknife game.

The punchline. Backing a trailer is unstable: the straight configuration is a saddle when reversing. You stabilize it by steering, but only up to a point. Beyond a purely geometric critical articulation angle, no amount of counter-steer can arrest the fold — the trailer jackknifes, and the only escape is to drive forward.

1. Model and assumptions

We work in the low-speed regime where tires roll without slipping, so each axle is a nonholonomic rolling constraint: its velocity is parallel to its own heading. No tire forces, no mass, no momentum — this is pure kinematics, and it is an excellent match to parking-lot speeds.

2. The tow vehicle: kinematic bicycle model

Collapse each axle to a single central wheel. The rear axle rolls along \(\theta_c\); the front wheel rolls along \(\theta_c+\delta\). Eliminating the instantaneous center of rotation gives the standard result

\[ \dot x = v\cos\theta_c,\qquad \dot y = v\sin\theta_c,\qquad \boxed{\;\dot\theta_c = \frac{v}{W}\tan\delta\;} \]

with \((x,y)\) the rear-axle point. The turn radius of the rear axle is \(R = W/\tan\delta\).

3. Adding the trailer: the articulation equation

The hitch ball sits a distance \(L\) behind the rear axle, so its position is \(\mathbf{p}_h = (x,y) - L(\cos\theta_c,\sin\theta_c)\). Its velocity is

\[ \dot{\mathbf p}_h = v(\cos\theta_c,\sin\theta_c) - L\dot\theta_c(-\sin\theta_c,\cos\theta_c). \]

The trailer axle is a distance \(D\) behind the hitch along \(\theta_t\). Its no-slip constraint says its velocity component perpendicular to \(\theta_t\) vanishes. The perpendicular speed of the hitch, expressed in the trailer frame, drives the trailer's rotation: resolving \(\dot{\mathbf p}_h\) onto the trailer's lateral direction and dividing by \(D\) gives

\[ \boxed{\;\dot\theta_t = -\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma \;-\; \frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\,\dot\theta_c\;} \]

Subtracting \(\dot\theta_c\) yields the single governing ODE for the articulation angle:

\[ \boxed{\;\dot\gamma = -\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma \;-\; \frac{v}{W}\!\left(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\right)\tan\delta\;} \]
The sign of the \(\sin\gamma\) term is the whole story. With \(\delta=0\), forward motion (\(v>0\)) gives \(\dot\gamma=-\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma\), which pushes \(\gamma\to 0\). Reverse (\(v<0\)) flips the sign and pushes \(\gamma\) away from \(0\). A trailer self-centers going forward and runs away going backward.

4. Stability of the straight configuration

Linearize about \(\gamma=0\) with \(\delta=0\):

\[ \dot\gamma \approx -\frac{v}{D}\,\gamma \;\Rightarrow\; \gamma(t)=\gamma_0\,e^{-(v/D)\,t}. \]

Forward (\(v>0\)): exponential decay, \(\gamma=0\) is asymptotically stable, time constant \(D/|v|\). Reverse (\(v<0\)): exponential growth, \(\gamma=0\) is unstable. This sign flip is exactly why backing a trailer is hard and driving forward is trivial.

5. The critical angle: when steering can no longer save you

While reversing you fight the instability with the steering term. Ask: for a given \(\gamma\), is there an admissible steer that makes \(\dot\gamma\le 0\) (i.e. arrests or reverses the fold)? Setting \(\dot\gamma=0\) and solving for the required steer,

\[ \tan\delta \;=\; -\,\frac{W}{D}\, \frac{\sin\gamma}{\,1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\,}. \]

The right side grows with \(\gamma\); the steer is bounded by \(|\delta|\le\delta_{\max}\). The largest holdable angle \(\gamma_c\) saturates the lock, \(\delta=\pm\delta_{\max}\):

\[ \boxed{\;\frac{\sin\gamma_c}{D} \;=\; \frac{\tan\delta_{\max}}{W}\left(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma_c\right)\;} \]

Two consequences worth emphasizing to students:

  • It is \(v\)-independent. Speed factors out of \(\dot\gamma=0\) entirely, so \(\gamma_c\) is a property of the geometry and steering limit alone. Crawling does not buy you a larger safe angle.
  • Beyond \(\gamma_c\) it is hopeless. For \(|\gamma|>\gamma_c\), even full opposite lock gives \(\dot\gamma\) the wrong sign, so \(|\gamma|\) grows monotonically to the physical contact angle. That is the jackknife.

The escape. Pulling forward (\(v>0\)) flips the unstable term to a stable one, so \(\gamma\) decays back toward zero regardless of how far it had folded (short of physical contact). "Pull forward to straighten" is not folklore; it is the sign of one term.

Worked number (the game's default rig)

For a Honda Odyssey with a small utility trailer: \(W=3.0\,\mathrm m\), \(L=1.1\,\mathrm m\), \(D=1.8\,\mathrm m\), \(\delta_{\max}=33^\circ\) (\(\tan\delta_{\max}=0.649\)). Solving the boxed transcendental equation numerically gives

\[ \gamma_c \approx 0.62\ \text{rad} \approx 35^\circ . \]

Shorten the trailer (smaller \(D\)) and \(\gamma_c\) shrinks — short trailers jackknife sooner, which is exactly why a small utility trailer is harder to back than a long camper.

6. Problem set

P1Derive the articulation ODE

Starting from the rolling (no-slip) constraints at the rear axle, front wheel, and trailer axle, derive \(\dot\theta_c=(v/W)\tan\delta\) and the boxed expression for \(\dot\gamma\). Identify precisely where the off-axle term \((L/D)\cos\gamma\) enters and show it vanishes for an on-axle hitch (\(L=0\)).

Show solution

Tow vehicle. Both axles roll without slipping, so each axle velocity is parallel to its own heading. With the rear axle at \((x,y)\) moving at speed \(v\) along \(\theta_c\), and the front axle a distance \(W\) ahead along \(\theta_c\) but rolling along \(\theta_c+\delta\), the two no-slip conditions share one instantaneous center of rotation. Eliminating it gives the bicycle result

\[ \dot x = v\cos\theta_c,\quad \dot y = v\sin\theta_c,\quad \dot\theta_c=\frac{v}{W}\tan\delta . \]

Hitch velocity. The hitch ball sits a distance \(L\) behind the rear axle, \(\mathbf p_h=(x,y)-L(\cos\theta_c,\sin\theta_c)\). Differentiating,

\[ \dot{\mathbf p}_h = v(\cos\theta_c,\sin\theta_c) - L\dot\theta_c(-\sin\theta_c,\cos\theta_c). \]

Trailer constraint. The trailer axle, a distance \(D\) behind the hitch along \(\theta_t\), may not slide sideways: the component of \(\dot{\mathbf p}_h\) perpendicular to \(\theta_t\) is what rotates the trailer, and it does so with moment arm \(D\). The unit lateral direction of the trailer is \((-\sin\theta_t,\cos\theta_t)\). Projecting,

\[ \dot{\mathbf p}_h\cdot(-\sin\theta_t,\cos\theta_t) = -v\sin(\theta_t-\theta_c)\;-\;L\dot\theta_c\cos(\theta_t-\theta_c), \]

where the cross terms collapse via \(\sin\theta_c\sin\theta_t+\cos\theta_c\cos\theta_t =\cos\gamma\) and \(\cos\theta_c\sin\theta_t-\sin\theta_c\cos\theta_t=\sin\gamma\), with \(\gamma=\theta_t-\theta_c\). Dividing the lateral speed by \(D\),

\[ \dot\theta_t=-\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma-\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\,\dot\theta_c . \]

Articulation ODE. Subtract \(\dot\theta_c\) and substitute \(\dot\theta_c=(v/W)\tan\delta\):

\[ \dot\gamma=\dot\theta_t-\dot\theta_c =-\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma-\frac{v}{W}\!\left(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\right)\tan\delta . \]

Where the off-axle term enters. The \((L/D)\cos\gamma\) factor is the fingerprint of the hitch overhang: it is exactly the \(-\tfrac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\, \dot\theta_c\) coupling, the trailer being yawed by the lateral swing of an off-axle hitch as the car turns. Setting \(L=0\) (hitch over the rear axle) kills this swing, the hitch velocity is then purely \(v(\cos\theta_c,\sin\theta_c)\), and

\[ \dot\gamma\big|_{L=0}=-\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma-\frac{v}{W}\tan\delta . \]

P2Linear stability

Linearize \(\dot\gamma\) about \(\gamma=0\) for \(\delta=0\) and show the straight configuration is asymptotically stable in forward motion and unstable in reverse. Give the growth/decay time constant. Interpret physically.

Show solution

Set \(\delta=0\). The ODE reduces to \(\dot\gamma=-\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma\), so \(\gamma=0\) is an equilibrium. Linearize with \(\sin\gamma\approx\gamma\):

\[ \dot\gamma\approx-\frac{v}{D}\,\gamma \;\Rightarrow\; \gamma(t)=\gamma_0\,e^{-(v/D)\,t}. \]

The single eigenvalue is \(\lambda=-v/D\).

  • Forward, \(v>0\): \(\lambda<0\), so \(\gamma\) decays exponentially. The straight configuration is asymptotically stable; small kinks heal themselves.
  • Reverse, \(v<0\): \(\lambda>0\), so \(\gamma\) grows exponentially. The straight configuration is unstable; any kink runs away.

The magnitude of the time constant is

\[ \tau=\frac{1}{|\lambda|}=\frac{D}{|v|}, \]

i.e. \(\gamma\) changes by a factor \(e\) over a path length \(|v|\tau=D\), one trailer length of travel. Physically, a trailer rolling forward is dragged straight by its hitch (like an arrow with its feathers behind the tip), while in reverse the same geometry pushes the trailer, an inverted pendulum that tips over: stability is the sign of \(v\), and the relevant clock ticks per trailer-length traveled, not per second, since speed cancels into \(\tau\propto 1/|v|\).

P3The critical angle

Derive the boxed condition for \(\gamma_c\). Prove it is independent of \(v\). Compute \(\partial\gamma_c/\partial D\) and \(\partial\gamma_c/\partial \delta_{\max}\) and state their signs; explain why a shorter trailer or a smaller steering lock is more dangerous.

Show solution

The condition. While reversing (\(v<0\)) the driver wants \(\dot\gamma\le0\) for \(\gamma>0\). Setting \(\dot\gamma=0\) and solving for the steer,

\[ \tan\delta=-\frac{W}{D}\frac{\sin\gamma}{1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma}. \]

For \(\gamma>0\) this calls for \(\delta<0\) (opposite lock). Its magnitude grows with \(\gamma\) but the steer saturates at \(|\delta|=\delta_{\max}\). The largest holdable angle is where the required steer just reaches the lock, \(\tan\delta=-\tan\delta_{\max}\):

\[ \frac{\sin\gamma_c}{D}=\frac{\tan\delta_{\max}}{W}\!\left(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma_c\right). \]

Independence of \(v\). The factor \(\dot\gamma\) is linear in \(v\): \(\dot\gamma=v\,g(\gamma,\delta)\) with \(g=-\frac{\sin\gamma}{D}-\frac{1}{W}(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma)\tan\delta\). For \(v\ne0\), \(\dot\gamma=0\iff g=0\), and \(g\) contains no \(v\). So the equilibrium condition, and hence \(\gamma_c\), is set by geometry and \(\delta_{\max}\) alone. Crawling does not enlarge the safe envelope; it only slows the clock \(\tau=D/|v|\) at which you approach it.

Sensitivities. Write the defining relation as \(F(\gamma_c,p)=\sin\gamma_c-\frac{D}{W}\tan\delta_{\max}-\frac{L}{W}\tan\delta_{\max} \cos\gamma_c=0\) and use implicit differentiation, \(\partial\gamma_c/\partial p=-F_p/F_{\gamma_c}\), with

\[ F_{\gamma_c}=\cos\gamma_c+\frac{L}{W}\tan\delta_{\max}\sin\gamma_c>0 \quad(\text{for } 0<\gamma_c<\tfrac{\pi}{2}). \]

For \(D\):

\[ F_D=-\frac{\tan\delta_{\max}}{W}<0 \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{\partial\gamma_c}{\partial D}=-\frac{F_D}{F_{\gamma_c}}>0 . \]

For \(\delta_{\max}\), with \(\frac{d}{d\delta_{\max}}\tan\delta_{\max}=\sec^2\delta_{\max}>0\):

\[ F_{\delta_{\max}}=-\frac{D+L\cos\gamma_c}{W}\sec^2\delta_{\max}<0 \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{\partial\gamma_c}{\partial\delta_{\max}}>0 . \]

Interpretation. Both derivatives are positive. A shorter trailer (smaller \(D\)) lowers \(\gamma_c\): the short lever folds fast, so the countersteer must win at a smaller angle, and the safe envelope shrinks (the page's utility-trailer-is-harder remark). A smaller steering lock (smaller \(\delta_{\max}\)) also lowers \(\gamma_c\): with less authority to bend the car the other way, you can hold less articulation before the fold escapes. Both make the rig more dangerous to back.

P4Phase line

Fix \(\delta=\delta_{\max}\) and \(v<0\). Sketch \(\dot\gamma\) versus \(\gamma\) on \([0,\pi/2]\). Locate the equilibria, classify their stability, and connect the unstable one to \(\gamma_c\). Add the \(\delta=0\) curve and discuss what the driver is doing by steering.

Show solution

Write \(v=-|v|\) and \(\delta=\delta_{\max}>0\) (full lock against the fold). Then

\[ \dot\gamma=\frac{|v|}{D}\sin\gamma+\frac{|v|}{W}\!\left(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\right) \tan\delta_{\max} \;\overset{?}{=}\;0 . \]

That sign pattern never vanishes for \(\gamma\in[0,\pi/2]\): both terms are positive, so this choice adds to the fold. The arresting steer is the opposite lock, \(\delta=-\delta_{\max}\), giving

\[ \dot\gamma(\gamma)=\frac{|v|}{D}\sin\gamma -\frac{|v|}{W}\!\left(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\right)\tan\delta_{\max}. \]

Shape. At \(\gamma=0\), \(\dot\gamma(0)=-\frac{|v|}{W}(1+L/D) \tan\delta_{\max}<0\): full opposite lock actively straightens the rig, even pushing \(\gamma\) slightly negative. As \(\gamma\) grows the positive \(\frac{|v|}{D}\sin\gamma\) term wins, and \(\dot\gamma\) rises through zero. There is exactly one root in \((0,\pi/2)\), call it \(\gamma^\ast\); it satisfies \(\frac{\sin\gamma^\ast}{D}=\frac{\tan\delta_{\max}}{W}(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma^\ast)\), which is precisely the boxed critical-angle condition, so \(\gamma^\ast=\gamma_c\).

dgamma/dt | (delta = +delta_max, full WRONG lock: all positive) | .-'' | .-' ----+----o------------*---------------- gamma | / gamma_c | _/ (delta = -delta_max, full opposite lock) |/ gamma=0

Equilibria and stability. For \(\delta=-\delta_{\max}\) the only root on \([0,\pi/2]\) is \(\gamma_c\), where \(\dot\gamma'(\gamma_c)=\frac{|v|}{D}\cos\gamma_c +\frac{|v|}{W}\frac{L}{D}\sin\gamma_c\tan\delta_{\max}>0\): the slope is positive, so \(\gamma_c\) is unstable. Below \(\gamma_c\) (\(\dot\gamma<0\)) the countersteer wins and the kink shrinks; above \(\gamma_c\) (\(\dot\gamma>0\)) it loses and \(\gamma\) runs to the contact angle. \(\gamma_c\) is the watershed.

The \(\delta=0\) curve. Without steering, \(\dot\gamma=\frac{|v|}{D}\sin\gamma\ge0\) on \([0,\pi/2]\), with its only equilibrium at \(\gamma=0\) and that equilibrium unstable (positive slope). Steering does two things at once: it shifts the whole curve downward by the steering term, turning a region of \(\dot\gamma>0\) into \(\dot\gamma<0\), and it pushes the dangerous crossing out from \(\gamma=0\) to \(\gamma=\gamma_c\). The driver is not removing the instability, they are buying angle, enlarging the basin of straightening from the single point \(\gamma=0\) to the interval \([0,\gamma_c)\).

P5A Lyapunov view

Consider \(V(\gamma)=1-\cos\gamma\ge 0\). Compute \(\dot V\) along trajectories for forward motion with \(\delta=0\) and show \(\dot V\le 0\), giving a Lyapunov proof that the trailer self-centers going forward. What goes wrong in reverse?

Show solution

Take \(V(\gamma)=1-\cos\gamma\). It is positive definite on \((-\pi,\pi)\): \(V(0)=0\) and \(V(\gamma)>0\) otherwise, a valid energy-like function with its well at the straight configuration. Along trajectories with \(\delta=0\), \(\dot\gamma=-\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma\), so

\[ \dot V=\frac{dV}{d\gamma}\,\dot\gamma =\sin\gamma\cdot\left(-\frac{v}{D}\sin\gamma\right) =-\frac{v}{D}\sin^2\gamma . \]

Forward (\(v>0\)). Then \(\dot V=-\frac{v}{D}\sin^2\gamma\le0\), with equality only at \(\gamma=0\) (and the boundary \(\gamma=\pi\)). \(V\) is positive definite and decreasing, so by Lyapunov's theorem \(\gamma=0\) is stable; since \(\dot V=0\) only on the equilibrium set, LaSalle gives asymptotic stability on \((-\pi,\pi)\). The trailer self-centers from any non-folded start, not merely from small angles, which is the global version of the linear P2 result.

Reverse (\(v<0\)). The sign flips: \(\dot V=+\frac{|v|}{D}\sin^2\gamma \ge0\). The would-be Lyapunov function now increases along trajectories everywhere except at \(\gamma=0\). \(V\) is a Chetaev-type function certifying instability: any perturbation off \(\gamma=0\) climbs the well outward, so the straight configuration is unstable and \(|\gamma|\) grows toward the contact angle. The same \(1-\cos\gamma\) that proves forward stability proves reverse instability simply by changing the sign of \(v\).

P6Off-tracking (steady turn)

In a constant-radius turn the articulation angle settles to \(\dot\gamma=0\). Find the steady \(\gamma\) and the trailer-axle turn radius as functions of \(\delta\). Show the trailer cuts inside the tow vehicle's path (off-tracking) and quantify the offset for a \(90^\circ\) turn.

Show solution

Steady articulation. Hold \(\delta\) fixed and forward (\(v>0\)). Set \(\dot\gamma=0\):

\[ \frac{\sin\gamma}{D}=-\frac{\tan\delta}{W}\!\left(1+\frac{L}{D}\cos\gamma\right). \]

(For \(\delta>0\) the steady \(\gamma\) is negative, the trailer trails to the inside; the sign just records that the trailer lags the turn.) For the common on-axle case \(L=0\) this collapses to the clean closed form

\[ \sin\gamma_{\mathrm{ss}}=-\frac{D}{W}\tan\delta . \]

Turn radii. The rear axle traces radius \(R_c=W/\tan\delta\). In steady state the trailer axle traces a concentric circle about the same center. Its radius is the distance from that center to the trailer axle. With the hitch at \(L=0\) the hitch rides on \(R_c\), and the trailer axle (distance \(D\) behind, perpendicular offset set by \(\gamma_{\mathrm{ss}}\)) traces

\[ R_t=\sqrt{R_c^{\,2}-D^2} =\sqrt{\left(\frac{W}{\tan\delta}\right)^{2}-D^2}\;<\;R_c . \]

(Including the overhang, the hitch circle has radius \(R_h=\sqrt{R_c^2+L^2}\) and \(R_t=\sqrt{R_h^2-D^2}=\sqrt{R_c^2+L^2-D^2}\).)

Off-tracking. Because \(R_t \[ \Delta R=R_c-R_t=R_c-\sqrt{R_c^2-D^2} \approx\frac{D^2}{2R_c}\quad(R_c\gg D). \]

A \(90^\circ\) turn. The offset above is the steady-state radial gap, and for a quarter-turn at constant radius the trailer's path is displaced inward of the tow vehicle's by this full \(\Delta R\) at the apex. Using the page's rig, \(W=3.0\,\mathrm m\), \(D=1.8\,\mathrm m\), and a moderate \(\delta=20^\circ\) (\(\tan\delta=0.364\)):

\[ R_c=\frac{3.0}{0.364}=8.24\,\mathrm m,\qquad R_t=\sqrt{8.24^2-1.8^2}=8.04\,\mathrm m, \] \[ \Delta R=8.24-8.04\approx0.20\,\mathrm m . \]

So the trailer wheels track about \(0.2\,\mathrm m\) inside the tow vehicle's wheels through the corner, the reason you must swing wide when towing. Tighter turns (smaller \(R_c\), larger \(\delta\)) and longer trailers (larger \(D\)) both inflate \(\Delta R\) roughly as \(D^2/2R_c\).

7. Notes on the simulation

The Jackknife game integrates the boxed \(\dot\gamma\) (and the car pose) with semi-implicit Euler at 120 Hz, stores both absolute headings, and computes \(\gamma_c\) once per rig by bisection on the boxed condition. When \(|\gamma|\) crosses \(\gamma_c\) the game disables reverse and prompts you to pull forward — the rule and the physics are the same object.
